Primary Mirror

Webb’s 6.5-meter primary mirror is made of 18 hexagonal segments of gold-coated beryllium. It collects enough light to see the first galaxies and the faint glow of exoplanets.

Size and design

The James Webb Space Telescope’s primary mirror is 6.5 meters (21.3 feet) in diameter—about 2.7 times the diameter of Hubble’s 2.4-meter mirror. That gives Webb more than seven times the light-collecting area of Hubble, so it can see fainter and more distant objects.

The mirror is made of 18 hexagonal segments. Hexagons pack together with no gaps, and the shape is easier to fold for launch. Each segment is about 1.3 meters across and weighs roughly 20 kilograms. They are made of beryllium, which is stiff, light, and stable at the cryogenic temperatures Webb needs for infrared observations.

Webb’s First Deep Field—possible because of the large mirror’s light-gathering power. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Gold coating

Each segment is coated with a thin layer of gold—about 0.1 micron thick—to maximize reflectivity in the infrared. Gold reflects more than 98% of infrared light in Webb’s wavelength range. The famous “gold mirror” look is a side effect of optimizing for science, not aesthetics.

Folding and deployment

The mirror was too large to fit inside the Ariane 5 rocket in its final shape. So it was built in three sections (a center section and two wings), and the two wings were folded back for launch. After launch, the wings were unfolded in space. Each segment has actuators so engineers can adjust the mirror’s shape with nanometer precision. This process, called wavefront sensing and control, turned the 18 segments into a single, precise optical surface.

Secondary mirror

Webb also has a secondary mirror—a convex hyperbola about 0.74 meters across—held in front of the primary by three struts. Light from the sky hits the primary, bounces to the secondary, and then goes into the instrument module behind the primary. This design (Cassegrain-style) keeps the instruments in a compact package and allows the sunshield to protect the whole telescope.
