Science Instruments

Webb carries four science instruments and a Fine Guidance Sensor. Together they cover near- and mid-infrared wavelengths to study the first galaxies, star birth, and exoplanet atmospheres.

Why infrared?

Webb is an infrared telescope. The first stars and galaxies emitted their light in ultraviolet and visible light, but that light has been stretched by the expansion of the universe into infrared by the time it reaches us. Dust that blocks visible light is more transparent in the infrared, and cool objects (like planets and disks) glow in the infrared. So to see the earliest universe and the birth of stars and planets, Webb had to be built for infrared.

Webb’s First Deep Field—infrared view of thousands of galaxies. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

The four science instruments

All four instruments sit in the Integrated Science Instrument Module (ISIM) behind the primary mirror. Light from the telescope is split and sent to the instruments. They can be used in parallel for some observations.

  • NIRCam (Near-Infrared Camera) — Webb’s primary imager. Takes pictures in near-infrared (0.6–5 µm), used for alignment of the mirror and for deep imaging. Details →
  • NIRSpec (Near-Infrared Spectrograph) — Splits light into spectra. Can observe up to 100 objects at once (multi-object spectroscopy). Details →
  • MIRI (Mid-Infrared Instrument) — Covers 5–28 µm. Provides imaging and spectroscopy in the mid-infrared for dust, cool stars, and distant galaxies. Details →
  • NIRISS (Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph) — Imaging and slitless spectroscopy; used for exoplanet transit spectroscopy and wide-field surveys. Details →

The Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) keeps the telescope pointed with extreme stability. It can also take images (FGS imaging mode) for engineering and calibration. Details →
