Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS)

The FGS keeps Webb pointed with extreme stability so that long exposures and precise spectroscopy are possible. It can also take images for calibration and engineering.

What the FGS does

The Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) is not a science instrument in the usual sense—its main job is to keep the telescope pointed at the right place in the sky with very high stability. Webb must hold a target to within a few milliarcseconds (thousandths of an arcsecond) so that light stays on the same pixels for minutes or hours. Without that stability, deep images would blur and spectra would be degraded.

The FGS uses two regions of its field of view to lock onto guide stars. It sends pointing error signals to the spacecraft’s attitude control system many times per second so that the telescope can make tiny corrections. This is why Webb can do such long, stable exposures and achieve its diffraction-limited resolution.

Sharp images like the First Deep Field depend on FGS keeping the telescope steady. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

FGS imaging mode

Although the FGS is primarily for guiding, it has an imaging capability. In FGS imaging mode, the instrument can take near-infrared images over a small field. This is used for calibration, for checking telescope alignment, and sometimes for science (e.g., very high-precision astrometry). The first “preview” image released from Webb during commissioning was from the FGS—a proof that the optics were working before the science instruments were fully commissioned.

Precise pointing enables complex mosaics like Stephan’s Quintet. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI
Long exposures on targets like the Southern Ring Nebula rely on FGS stability. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Canadian contribution

The FGS was designed and built by the Canadian Space Agency (CSA) and is part of the same hardware unit as NIRISS. Canada’s contribution to Webb is the FGS-NIRISS assembly, which provides both guiding and NIRISS science capability.
