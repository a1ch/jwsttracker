NIRSpec

The Near-Infrared Spectrograph splits light into spectra so scientists can measure the composition, motion, and distance of galaxies and stars. It can observe up to 100 objects at once.

What NIRSpec does

NIRSpec (Near-Infrared Spectrograph) is Webb’s primary spectrograph for the near-infrared range (0.6–5.3 µm). Instead of taking pretty pictures, a spectrograph spreads light into a spectrum—a rainbow of wavelengths. From that spectrum, astronomers can measure the chemical composition of gas and dust, the motion of stars and galaxies (via Doppler shift), and the redshift (and thus distance) of the first galaxies.

NIRSpec has three main modes: multi-object spectroscopy (observing many targets at once using a microshutter array), fixed-slit spectroscopy (a single slit for bright targets), and integral field unit (IFU) spectroscopy (a 3×3 arcsecond field split into spatial and spectral information).

Galaxies in Webb’s First Deep Field are prime targets for NIRSpec spectroscopy to measure composition and distance. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Microshutter array

One of NIRSpec’s most innovative features is its microshutter array: a grid of about 250,000 tiny shutters, each the width of a human hair. Scientists can open only the shutters that align with objects they want to observe (e.g., dozens of galaxies in a single field), and keep the rest closed to block sky background. That way NIRSpec can collect spectra of up to 100 objects simultaneously, making surveys of distant galaxies much faster than observing one at a time.

Stephan’s Quintet—NIRSpec can target individual regions for spectroscopy. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI
Sombrero Galaxy—NIRSpec can probe chemical makeup of different areas. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Who built it

NIRSpec was built for ESA (European Space Agency) by Airbus with the microshutter array supplied by NASA Goddard. It is one of two ESA-provided instruments on Webb (the other is MIRI, built in partnership with NASA).
