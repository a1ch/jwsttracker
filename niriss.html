NIRISS

The Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph provides imaging and slitless spectroscopy. It is especially powerful for exoplanet atmospheres and wide-field surveys.

What NIRISS does

NIRISS (Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph) is a Canadian Space Agency (CSA) instrument on Webb. It covers the near-infrared from about 0.8 to 5.0 µm and has several modes: imaging, wide-field slitless spectroscopy (WFSS), single-object slitless spectroscopy (SOSS), and aperture masking interferometry (AMI).

NIRISS is particularly well suited for exoplanet transit spectroscopy. When a planet passes in front of its star, some starlight filters through the planet’s atmosphere. NIRISS’s SOSS mode spreads that light into a spectrum, revealing which molecules (water, CO₂, etc.) are in the atmosphere. Webb’s first spectrum of an exoplanet atmosphere (WASP-96 b) was taken with NIRISS.

NIRISS can do slitless spectroscopy across crowded fields like deep fields. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Slitless spectroscopy

“Slitless” means NIRISS doesn’t use a narrow slit to select one object. Instead, it uses a grism (a combination of a prism and a grating) to spread all the light in the field into spectra. That is ideal for fields with many objects (e.g., deep fields or star clusters) where you want spectra of everything at once, and for exoplanet transits where the star and planet are in the same beam.

Galaxies and star fields are targets for NIRISS wide-field spectroscopy. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI
Star-forming regions can be studied with NIRISS imaging and spectroscopy. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Canadian contribution

NIRISS was designed and built in Canada (CSA, with Honeywell and others). It shares the same focal plane assembly as the Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS)—they are in the same unit, with NIRISS taking over when the telescope is pointed at a science target and FGS is not needed for guiding.
