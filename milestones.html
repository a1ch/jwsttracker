Key Milestones

From concept to launch to first light: the major steps in the James Webb Space Telescope’s journey.

Origins and development

Webb was proposed in the 1990s as a successor to the Hubble Space Telescope, with a focus on infrared astronomy to see the first galaxies. Development involved NASA, the European Space Agency (ESA), and the Canadian Space Agency (CSA). The telescope is named after James E. Webb, NASA’s administrator during the Apollo era. Building the observatory took decades and required new technology: lightweight beryllium mirrors, a deployable sunshield, and instruments that could work at cryogenic temperatures in space.

Webb’s First Deep Field—the kind of science the mission was designed for. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

December 25, 2021 — Launch

Webb launched on Christmas Day 2021 aboard an Ariane 5 rocket from Europe’s spaceport in Kourou, French Guiana. The launch was a partnership with ESA, which provided the Ariane 5 and the launch campaign. The telescope was folded to fit inside the rocket’s fairing; the next few weeks would be devoted to unfolding it in space. The launch was so accurate that it saved fuel for a longer science lifetime.

January 2022 — Deployment and cruise to L2

In the days and weeks after launch, Webb executed a series of critical deployments: sunshield deployment, mirror wing deployment, and alignment of the 18 mirror segments. Each step had to work the first time—there are no repair missions at L2. By the end of January 2022, the telescope had arrived at the Sun–Earth L2 point and entered its halo orbit. The sunshield was keeping the optics and instruments in shadow, and the observatory began cooling down.

Spring–Summer 2022 — Commissioning

Through the spring and early summer of 2022, the team completed commissioning: aligning the mirror segments using NIRCam, calibrating all four science instruments and the FGS, and verifying that the telescope met its performance requirements. In June 2022, the first “preview” image from the Fine Guidance Sensor showed that the optics were working. By July, the observatory was declared ready for science.

July 12, 2022 — First full-color images

On July 12, 2022, NASA, ESA, and CSA released the first full-color science images and spectra from Webb. They included Webb’s First Deep Field (SMACS 0723), the Cosmic Cliffs in the Carina Nebula, Stephan’s Quintet, the Southern Ring Nebula, and the spectrum of the exoplanet WASP-96 b. The images showed that Webb was not only working but exceeding expectations—revealing detail and depth that had never been seen before.

Later iconic images, like the Sombrero Galaxy, built on the success of first light. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

2022–Present — Science operations

Since July 2022, Webb has been in full science operations. Astronomers around the world propose observations, and approved programs use the telescope to study the first galaxies, star and planet formation, exoplanet atmospheres, and the solar system. New images and results are released regularly. The mission is designed to last at least 5 years, with a goal of 10 or more; fuel for station-keeping at L2 could allow more than 20 years of operation.
