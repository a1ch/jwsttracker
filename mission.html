Mission at a Glance

The James Webb Space Telescope is the largest, most powerful space observatory ever built. It sees the universe in infrared to peer at the first galaxies, stars, and exoplanets.

Distance from Earth

Webb orbits the Sun–Earth L2 Lagrange point, roughly 1.5 million kilometers (about 1 million miles) from Earth. At that distance, radio signals take about five seconds to travel each way. The telescope stays in a halo orbit around L2, which keeps it in a stable position relative to Earth and the Sun so the sunshield can always block heat and light from both.

Webb’s First Deep Field (SMACS 0723)—one of the first images from L2. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Because Webb is so far from Earth, it cannot be serviced by astronauts like Hubble was. Every system was designed for reliability and tested on the ground before launch.

Orbit location: L2

The second Sun–Earth Lagrange point (L2) is one of five equilibrium points where the combined gravity of the Sun and Earth allows a spacecraft to “park” with minimal fuel. Webb orbits around L2 in a halo orbit rather than sitting exactly at the point. This keeps the telescope cold and stable while allowing continuous communication with Earth.

Primary mirror: 6.5 meters

Webb’s primary mirror is 6.5 meters (21.3 feet) across—about 2.7 times the diameter of Hubble’s mirror. It is made of 18 hexagonal segments of gold-coated beryllium. The large size collects more light so Webb can see fainter, more distant objects. The mirror had to fold to fit inside the Ariane 5 rocket and then unfold in space.

Five science instruments

Webb carries four science instruments plus a Fine Guidance Sensor (FGS) that keeps the telescope pointed with extreme precision. Together they cover near-infrared and mid-infrared wavelengths so scientists can study the first galaxies, star and planet formation, and exoplanet atmospheres.

  • NIRCam — Near-Infrared Camera (primary imager)
  • NIRSpec — Near-Infrared Spectrograph (multi-object spectroscopy)
  • MIRI — Mid-Infrared Instrument (imaging and spectroscopy)
  • NIRISS — Near-Infrared Imager and Slitless Spectrograph
  • FGS — Fine Guidance Sensor (pointing)
