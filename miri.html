MIRI

The Mid-Infrared Instrument sees wavelengths from 5 to 28 micrometers. It reveals cool dust, planet-forming disks, and the hearts of dusty galaxies that near-infrared light cannot penetrate.

What MIRI does

MIRI (Mid-Infrared Instrument) is the only instrument on Webb that observes in the mid-infrared, from 5 to 28 micrometers (µm). At these longer wavelengths, we see cooler objects: dust glowing from starlight, planet-forming disks, cool stars, and the obscured centers of galaxies. MIRI has both an imager and a spectrograph, so it can take pictures and spread light into spectra.

Because mid-infrared is thermal (heat) radiation, MIRI must be colder than the other instruments. It has a dedicated cryocooler that chills it to about 7 Kelvin (−266 °C), colder than the rest of ISIM. That makes MIRI one of the most complex and carefully engineered parts of Webb.

Sombrero Galaxy—combined NIRCam and MIRI view. MIRI highlights dust and cooler structures. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Science with MIRI

MIRI is used to study protoplanetary disks (where planets form), dusty star-forming galaxies, the regions around supermassive black holes, cool brown dwarfs, and the chemistry of ices and organics in molecular clouds. When combined with NIRCam or NIRSpec data, MIRI gives a fuller picture from near- to mid-infrared.

Partnership

MIRI was developed jointly by NASA and ESA, with the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) leading the U.S. contribution and a European consortium providing the optical assembly and other components. The cryocooler was a major engineering challenge to bring MIRI down to 7 K in space.
