NIRCam

The Near-Infrared Camera is Webb’s primary imager. It sees light from 0.6 to 5 micrometers and was used to align the 18 mirror segments. Many of Webb’s most famous images come from NIRCam.

What NIRCam does

NIRCam (Near-Infrared Camera) is one of four science instruments on the James Webb Space Telescope. It is the primary near-infrared imager, covering wavelengths from about 0.6 to 5 micrometers (µm). NIRCam has two identical optical modules, each covering a portion of the field of view, so together they image a large area of the sky with high resolution.

Besides science imaging, NIRCam was essential during commissioning: it was used for wavefront sensing and control—the process that aligned the 18 primary mirror segments into a single, precise optical surface. Without NIRCam, Webb could not have achieved its sharp focus.

Webb’s First Deep Field (SMACS 0723)—a NIRCam-dominated image showing thousands of galaxies. Credit: NASA, ESA, CSA, STScI

Science with NIRCam

NIRCam is used for deep fields (like the First Deep Field), imaging of galaxies, star-forming regions, and exoplanet systems. It has coronagraphic masks to block bright starlight so fainter companions and disks can be seen. It also supports slitless spectroscopy in combination with other instruments. Many of Webb’s iconic early images—the Cosmic Cliffs, Stephan’s Quintet, the Southern Ring—rely heavily on NIRCam data.

Technical details

NIRCam was built by the University of Arizona with Lockheed Martin. It uses mercury-cadmium-telluride (HgCdTe) detectors and has multiple filters and coronagraphs. The two modules provide redundancy and a wide field of view (about 2.2 × 2.2 arcminutes per module). NIRCam can work at the same time as NIRSpec and NIRISS for parallel observations.
